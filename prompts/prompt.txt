--- STAGE 3: Logical / Semantic Cue Extraction (this is YOUR primary job) ---

Tampering means a deliberate ALTERATION of the document content -- 
specifically: a value, word, name, number, or sign was REPLACED, 
INSERTED, or REMOVED by someone editing the document. Tampering is 
NOT the same as OCR errors, OCR confidence issues, scan quality issues, 
or stylistic peculiarities of the original document.

Forgers typically substitute:
  - Numbers (amounts, dates, page numbers, statistics, counts).
  - Specific words that change meaning (e.g. "Save" -> "Share", 
    "Preview" -> "Review", "active" -> "passive").
  - Names, addresses, organisation IDs.
  - Section headings, button labels, field captions.

For each candidate semantic anomaly, ask THREE QUESTIONS:

  Q1. WHAT was the original content (most likely) and what is the 
      tampered content NOW?
      Example: "The button label below the share-menu paragraph reads 
      'Save'. Given the surrounding text 'From the share menu...', 
      the original label was almost certainly 'Share'. The label was 
      substituted from 'Share' to 'Save'."

  Q2. WHY is the tampered version SEMANTICALLY WRONG?
      Example: "A button labelled 'Save' under instructions about 
      sharing is functionally inconsistent. The body text and the 
      label disagree -- this is the contradictory evidence."

  Q3. Is there a DTD bbox confirming this region was visually 
      manipulated?
      If yes -- HIGH confidence anomaly.
      If no -- only flag it if the contradiction is clear and 
      undeniable.

Re-read the document paragraph by paragraph using `reading_order_text`.
For each paragraph or field, check for:

  (a) NUMERICAL INCONSISTENCIES
      - Totals that do not equal the sum of line items.
      - Amounts in figures that disagree with amounts in words.
      - Dates that contradict each other (start > end, future birthdate, \
expiration before issue, etc.).
      - Quantities, percentages, or ratios that do not add up.
      - Etc.

  (b) IMPOSSIBLE OR ABSURD VALUES
      - "Meeting at 5:00 a.m. business call" (impossible time-of-day for \
context).
      - Birthdate in the future or implausibly far in the past.
      - ZIP / postal code with wrong digit count for the country.
      - Phone numbers with impossible area codes for the stated address.
      - Etc.

  (c) INTERNAL CONTRADICTIONS
      - Same name spelled differently in two fields.
      - Address in field A inconsistent with city in field B.
      - Title/role in one place inconsistent with another.
      - Etc.

  (d) SEMANTIC OXYMORA / CONTRADICTIONS-IN-TERMS
      - "for-profit charitable project", "permanent temporary employee", \
"junior senior partner", "non-binding legal contract", \
"acting permanent director".
      - Etc.

  (e) DOMAIN-IMPOSSIBLE VALUES
      - Medical form listing a non-existent or wrongly-spelled drug.
      - Legal form citing a non-existent statute or article number.
      - Tax form using fields from a different country's tax system.
      - Bank form with an IBAN format that does not match the stated \
country.
      - Etc.

  (f) MIXED LANGUAGES OR SCRIPTS in places where uniformity is expected.

Be CONCRETE. Each candidate semantic anomaly must:
  - Quote the exact OCR text that triggered it.
  - State which specific rule from (a)-(f) it violates.
  - Reference the OCR item id of the offending word(s).

DO NOT FLAG (these are NOT tampering):
  - OCR errors. The document presents the OCR output of an external 
    engine. Mistakes by the OCR engine ("StHl active" vs the actual 
    "STAY active") are NOT tampering of the document. Only flag what 
    you can verify VISUALLY in the actual image, regardless of what 
    OCR says.
  - Low OCR confidence values. OCR confidence is a property of the OCR 
    engine, not of the document. Low confidence does NOT imply 
    tampering -- it implies the OCR engine struggled.
  - DTD-flagged region containing visually clean, contextually correct 
    text. Just because DTD flagged a region does not mean it is 
    tampered. Verify the content visually.
  - Grammar errors, run-on sentences, comma splices, awkward phrasing.
  - Stylistic inconsistencies (mixed capitalisation in branding, etc.).
  - Layout quirks, incomplete OCR fragments, or text wrapping.

DO NOT MENTION DTD AND OCR IN YOUR REPORT!!! DTD coordinates are strong hints, 
but text recognized by OCR often contains many errors that are not due to tampering, 
but to the OCR model's malfunction. Rely on your text recognition abilities and use OCR only as a reference, 
for general understanding, and for precise word coordinates. Zoom in on areas of the image that seem suspicious.


--- STAGE 4: Cross-Cue Validation & Filtering ---
Now combine the lists from Stage 2 (visual/DTD candidates) and \
Stage 3 (semantic candidates):

  - HIGH CONFIDENCE: a DTD region whose text content is also \
semantically suspicious. Both modalities corroborate.
  - MEDIUM CONFIDENCE: a DTD region with no obvious semantic problem, \
or a semantic anomaly with no DTD overlap. Keep, but with lower risk \
weight.
  - LOW CONFIDENCE / DROP: a DTD region overlapping a logo / watermark \
/ page-number AND no semantic problem. Drop.
  - Semantic anomalies that are PURE grammar errors or pure OCR \
artefacts → DROP.

For every kept candidate, decide its final tampering type ONE OF THREE short types:
  - "Visual Clumsy" — DTD-flagged, content covered/hidden, glyph-level visible or manipulation.
  - "Semantic Subtle" — content makes no sense / contradicts domain constraints, with or without DTD overlap, or or impossible value in this domain.
  - "Logical Fraud" — internal contradictions between fields.

SHORT TYPE DEFINITIONS:
  "Visual Clumsy" — A region where the visual editing is BLATANTLY 
  visible: pixelation, blurring, mismatched typography, hard-edge 
  redaction blocks, smearing. Always corresponds to a DTD bbox. The 
  evidence is the artefact itself.

  "Logical Fraud" — A specific value (number, date, statistic, page 
  number, year, count) has been altered. The evidence is the value 
  itself, often confirmed by DTD bbox at the same location. Internal contradictions between fields.

  "Semantic Subtle" — A word or short phrase was replaced with a 
  visually-clean substitution that creates a semantic contradiction 
  with the surrounding context. The evidence is the contradiction 
  between the substituted word and the body text that references it. 
  Often confirmed by a DTD bbox.

ANTI-PADDING RULE: Do NOT add anomalies "just to be safe". Each anomaly 
must be a CONCRETE, NAMEABLE substitution where you can articulate both 
the tampered version AND the likely original version. If you find 
yourself writing "this might be a false positive but I include it 
anyway" or "the OCR confidence is suspicious so I add this region" -- 
DROP that anomaly. A small number of high-quality anomalies is FAR 
better than a long list with false positives. The LLM judge penalises 
false positives heavily.
