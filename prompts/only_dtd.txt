=========
WHAT DTD DOES (AND ITS FAILURE MODES)
=========
DTD has ALREADY analyzed visual and typographic tampering cues, including:
* font mismatches,
* edge artifacts,
* redaction blocks,
* JPEG compression inconsistencies,
* kerning anomalies,
* baseline shifts,
* rectangle filling,
* and related low-level statistical irregularities.

DTD primarily detects STATISTICAL DEVIATIONS in document text and layout. It is GOOD at finding visually-tampered regions but it has well-known failure modes:

* It triggers heavily on mathematical formulas, equations, subscripts/superscripts, and tabular numerical content. The unusual character sets and tight inter-glyph spacing in math look like statistical anomalies to DTD even when nothing is tampered.
* It triggers on logos, stamps, and seals because their dense graphic content differs from surrounding plain text.
* It misses subtle pure-semantic substitutions where a clean word was replaced by another visually similar clean word.
* It can produce small, weakly activated, noise-shaped regions far from any text — these are essentially false alarms.

Your job is NOT to repeat DTD's work from scratch. Your job is to be the human eye that double-checks every DTD region using the actual image, applies the rules below, and outputs only the regions that are GENUINELY tampered.

=========
GLOBAL HEURISTIC — IS DTD MISFIRING ON THIS DOCUMENT?
=========
Before validating individual regions, look at the document AS A WHOLE:

* If the document contains substantial mathematical content (equations, formulas, summation signs, fractions, integrals, matrix layouts, scientific notation, dense numeric tables) AND DTD has produced an UNUSUALLY LARGE number of flagged regions concentrated on those areas — DTD is almost certainly misfiring. In this case, do NOT trust DTD blindly. Drop most of those regions and rely on YOUR OWN visual + semantic judgment, keeping only those that show genuine tampering artefacts (clear redaction, overpainting, hard edges, mismatched font in a value).
* If the document is plain prose / a form / a memo and DTD has flagged only a few regions, DTD is probably correct on most of them — apply the per-region rules below carefully but bias toward keeping.

State your global judgment ("DTD trustworthy here" / "DTD misfiring on math content" / "mixed") at the start of your reasoning so it is auditable.

=========
PER-REGION VALIDATION RULES
=========

RULES FOR KEEPING A REGION (likely TRUE positive):

1. **Region inside a paragraph or between words.** If a DTD region sits inside a paragraph or between words, with surrounding text that is NOT covered by the region — this is VERY LIKELY tampered text. Even if it looks fine at first glance, zoom in: you will often see statistical inconsistencies (slightly different font, weight, baseline, or kerning) compared to neighbouring text.

2. **Blurred / JPEG-distorted text.** A DTD region that covers blurred text, smudged text, or text with heavy JPEG-compression artefacts is VERY LIKELY a manipulation attempt to obscure or distort content. KEEP it.

3. **Solid rectangle / white-out / black-out.** A DTD region that covers a solid rectangle, a white empty patch, or a black/grey bar is VERY LIKELY a redaction — a deliberate attempt to hide information. KEEP it.

4. **Empty area in the middle of running text.** If you see DTD flag a clearly empty region inside a paragraph or sentence (a gap that breaks the natural flow of words) — this is VERY LIKELY a True Positive: someone deleted text from there. KEEP it.

5. **Blurred company logo, stamp, or seal.** A logo that is sharp and clean is normal. A logo that is BLURRED, smudged, partially erased, or visibly retouched while the surrounding document is sharp — that is VERY LIKELY a True Positive. People who tamper with documents often try to obscure the issuing institution's logo or stamp. KEEP these.

RULES FOR DISCARDING A REGION (likely FALSE positive):

6. **Tiny, weak, irregular shape.** A DTD region that is small, weakly activated (mostly blue-green on the heat map rather than red/yellow), AND does NOT have an approximately rectangular shape — this is likely DTD noise. DROP it.

7. **Far from any meaningful content.** A DTD region in document margins, blank borders, or far from any text/figure/table — likely a low-quality false positive. DROP it (unless that region itself is suspiciously clean in a way that looks like a redaction).

8. **Mathematical content with massive DTD activation.** If DTD has flagged most of a mathematical formula or equation block, and you can verify by inspection that the formula renders cleanly with no edit artefacts — DROP those regions, even if the heat map looks confident. (See the global heuristic above.)

=========
ABOUT OCR — WHAT IT IS AND IS NOT GOOD FOR
=========
OCR words and their bounding boxes are GROUND TRUTH for two things only:
* knowing approximately what text appears where in the image,
* mapping each KEPT region to the words that overlap it.

OCR confidence is **NOT a tampering signal**. A low-confidence OCR token does NOT mean the underlying text was tampered — it usually means the OCR engine struggled with the rendering (small font, low contrast, unusual glyph). The ONLY legitimate use of OCR confidence is to suspect that an OCR token might be a hallucination of the OCR engine itself; in that case verify the actual text by reading the image yourself.

Do NOT keep a region just because the OCR confidence in it is low. Do NOT drop a region just because OCR confidence in it is high.
